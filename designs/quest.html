Concept 02

The Quest.

Your child is the hero of their own RPG. Today's chores are quest pins on a map. Their pet is a leveling companion with stats. Screen time is rest tokens earned at the tavern. Parents are the dungeon master, posting side quests and dropping rare loot. Sibling rivalry becomes a co-op party. Identity is the engine: who am I? instead of what do I have to do?

Children spend hundreds of hours leveling up Pokémon, Animal Crossing villagers, and Stardew Valley farms. They are not allergic to chores — they are allergic to chores presented as paperwork. The Quest is what happens when you treat the family as a guild.

Below: four screens covering the full app surface. Today's quests (overworld map), pet management (companion menu), gaming/VR/screen-time (the tavern), and the parent's command center (Quest Master HQ).

A map. A hero. Three pins. Identity over admin.

The map is the home screen

Drop the kid into a pixel-art overworld where their character stands in the centre. Quest pins represent today's chores — yellow pins are side quests, red are urgent (overdue), blue are story chains. The character literally walks across the screen toward whatever the kid taps.

Class system

Each kid picks a "class" at signup: Scout (mobility/initiative), Cleric (helping siblings), Mage (academics), Bard (creativity). Class colors their HUD and unlocks specific quest types. It's cosmetic + a tiny stat boost — the point is identity, not balance. A 5-year-old who chooses Scout will introduce themselves as a Scout for weeks.

Quest chains

The "morning routine" is no longer a category — it's a multi-step quest with a story. "Wake the dragon (brush teeth) → tame the beast (make bed) → break your fast." Each step is one of the existing daily habits, but the kid sees a chain that wants finishing.

Edge cases
  • Empty day = the map shows the hero relaxing at camp. Idle animation. No urgent state.
  • Lots of quests = the camera zooms out. The map gets bigger as the day gets busier; never a list.
  • Quest declined = the pin stays on the map but greys out. Not gone, just not done. Becomes available the next day for a small coin penalty.
What we cut
  • Tabs (replaced by zones on the map: Home / Pet / Tavern / Shop / Profile)
  • The word "chore"
  • The "Quests" tab name (it's the whole app now)
  • Streak meters (replaced by the kid's XP bar — the same thing, named better)

Your pet has stats. Your pet levels up.

The pet is a party member

Tap your pet on the map and you enter the Companion menu. Fifi has Hunger, Happiness, Cleanliness, and Bond XP. Her stats decay slowly when she's not cared for; spike up when you tap Feed / Litter / Play. She levels up alongside you. After 30 days she becomes a "Royal Cat" with new portrait art.

Why this is a fundamentally different feature

The current "Feed Fifi" chore is a row in a list. Here, Fifi changes. Her hunger bar visibly drops over the day. The kid pulls up the menu mid-afternoon and sees Fifi at 30% hunger and feels something. That's the shift from compliance to care.

Multi-pet, multi-kid

Each kid has bond XP with each pet. Whoever feeds Fifi today gets bond XP with her — so Fifi's bond level is per-kid, not global. Over time, kids develop a "main pet". The 8-year-old loves Fifi and is Bond Lv 12; the 6-year-old is Bond Lv 4. Pet picks favourites based on who feeds them.

Rotation, made of consequence

The current rotation_children array becomes invisible mechanics. Whoever's turn it is gets the "FEED FIFI" quest pin on their map. If they skip, hunger drops further and tomorrow's pin is "FIFI IS HUNGRY!" — louder, redder, urgent. No nagging UI; the pet does the nagging by being hungry.

Setup (parent)

"Add a companion" → name, species, art (cat/dog/bird/dragon for kids who want fantasy). Pick which kids are bonded. That's all the configuration. Days-of-week schedules disappear — the pet is always there, the rotation rotates itself.

Screen time, framed as adventurer's rest.

Rest is earned, not given

The tavern is the kid's screen-time hub. The innkeeper greets them, announces rest tokens earned today, and points to four chambers — each a device category. Tap a chamber → real iOS Screen Time unlocks the relevant app on the device.

VR is the portal

VR isn't a "hours" toggle in a settings screen. It's a swirling purple portal at the back of the tavern, gated behind weekend days, after morning chores. Visually distinct, narratively special. Kids will look forward to it.

Per-device buckets

The current parental rule is a multi-axis matrix (kid × device × days × hours). Here it's four chambers. Each shows minutes remaining. Buckets refill nightly. Rules collapse into defaults:

  • Sitting room (TV) = free, no quota — TV doesn't go into a kid's pocket.
  • Games room (console) = 1h weekday / 2h weekend default.
  • Scrolling nook (tablet) = strictest, 30m weekday default.
  • VR portal = weekends only, locked behind morning quest chain.

Parents tap defaults to override per kid. Five toggles instead of the current 6×7 grid. Most families will keep defaults.

Earning the tokens

"Rest tokens" map directly to chore points (the existing data layer). 1 chore ≈ 15 minutes of rest. The kid sees this on the innkeeper's chalkboard so the bridge from "did chores → earned screens" is loud and obvious.

Empty tavern state

If the kid hasn't earned any rest, the rooms show "LOCKED" and the innkeeper says "Hero, return after some quests. Rest is earned." Friction with a smile, not a lecture.

Parents are dungeon masters, not auditors.

You are the DM

The parent's interface looks like a board-game master's command panel. Top: your party of heroes. Middle: pending quest completions (the approval queue). Bottom: action buttons to issue new quests or drop rare loot.

"Issue side quest" replaces "post contract"

The current Contracts board becomes "Side quests" — a parent posts an unscheduled task ("Vacuum the car for ★50"), kids see it as a glowing pin on their map. The economy is the same; the framing is RPG.

"Drop rare loot" is the gift mechanic

Parent can drop a rare item into a kid's map at any time — a new avatar item, an XP boost, a "skip a chore" token. This is the digital equivalent of a parent slipping a kid a fiver for being helpful. Discretionary, parent-discretionary, magical.

Multi-kid at-a-glance

The party row at the top shows every kid's level + class. Tap to drill into their map. Three taps deep instead of seven.

Co-op moments

If the parent participates (rotation toggle), they appear in the party as the DM with a separate "DM quest" line. They can also join a kid's quest as an ally — turns chores into "co-op missions" for kids who'd rather not do it alone. The shared support feature from the existing app, restyled.

Settings, hidden

No settings tab. Everything family-wide goes in a small "DM Codex" icon at the top corner: invite codes, role changes, screen- time defaults, family code, Stripe billing eventually. Three taps deep, where settings belong.

What's still on the table

  • Rewards / shop → "blacksmith + armory". Spend coins on avatar gear (helmets, capes, mounts), consumable potions (small XP boosts), or real-world rewards posted by the parent.
  • Skills profile → already RPG-natural. Six skills become six stat lines on the character sheet — Discipline, Bond, Initiative, Reliability, Teamwork, Innovation. Visible on the Profile zone.
  • Bin collection rotation → a "village errand" with a wheelbarrow icon — appears as a rotating side quest, given to whoever's turn.
  • Streak / weekly badges → guild ranks. Stay 7 days streaking → "Initiate" rank. 30 days → "Veteran". Annual → "Champion of the Guild".
