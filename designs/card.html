Concept 03

The Card.

Your day is a stack of cards. The top card is the only thing that matters. Do the thing. Swipe up. The next card slides into place. The whole app is one screen, one card, one tap. Every other surface — pets, screen time, rewards, family — uses the same primitive: a card to act on, then the next.

Things 3 sold half a million copies of a to-do app at $9.99 because it respects you. iA Writer became a category on the strength of typography alone. The Card concept asks: what if a chore app was actually a tool, not a game? Premium. Calm. Adult-feeling. And quietly aspirational for older kids — they want to be treated like adults, not like preschoolers.

Below: four screens covering the full app surface. Today's chores (the stack), pet management (a pet card), screen time (a budget card with progress ring), and the parent's family view (deck rack).

One thing at a time.

The whole interface is one card

The kid never sees more than one chore at a time. The stack underneath signals "there's more"; the counter top-right tells the truth ("3 / 5 today"). Nothing else lives on this screen. No tabs, no leaderboard, no XP, no level, no badges, no streak. The only rendered number is "3 / 5".

Two ways to complete

Either swipe the card up (gesture, fast, satisfying) or tap the big black "Done" button at the bottom (deliberate, accessible). Either fires a heavy haptic, a single warm "ting" sound, and slides the next card up. The whole app is one button.

Cards are typed by colour
  • Daily habit cards = warm yellow accent (the toothbrush card above).
  • Routine cards (pets, bins) = green accent.
  • Household cards = neutral white.
  • Pitched / contract cards = purple accent.

The kid learns the language fast — they see colour and know what category they're about. No icons in the corner. No tags. The card IS the category.

Empty stack

When the kid completes their last card, the screen goes blank cream paper with one line in the centre: "That's all today. Well done." And then, intentionally, nothing. No dashboard appears. No "what's next" pop-up. The app respectfully closes the door — the kid puts down the phone, which is the entire point.

Long-press to peek

Long-press the card → a peek modal slides up showing the full stack of today's cards as a spread (like Apple Wallet). Tap any to do it out of order; the front-of-stack reorders. Hold-and-drag to re-prioritize. Power user gesture; doesn't clutter the default.

What we cut
  • Tabs (now 0)
  • Numbers, except "3 / 5"
  • Colour palette: cream paper, ink black, three accent hues. That's it.
  • The shop, leaderboard, schedule, profile screens — all reachable, never visible by default.

Pet care reuses the card primitive.

Pets are cards too

When it's the kid's turn to care for Fifi, a green-accented card rises to the front of the stack. Same gesture, same haptic, same button — the pet card just has additional context: a pet portrait, a "mood" line in plain English, and three thin status rings showing her current Hunger / Litter / Water levels.

Plain English, not stat bars

"She's been waiting by her bowl for 20 minutes" is the headline. The status rings are secondary, almost decorative — they exist for kids who like detail. Most kids will read the sentence and tap Done. The data is there if they care; not in their face if they don't.

Last fed by Sara at 7:42

The card surfaces who in the family last did each pet task. This creates ambient awareness without a "rotation schedule" UI. The current `walk_rotation_children: []` array still drives the "whose turn" assignment under the hood, but the kid never sees a checkbox grid.

The pet itself isn't a screen

There's no separate "Fifi profile" page. Fifi shows up as a card when she needs you. When she doesn't, she's not on screen. The pet exists in the moments when she matters.

Multi-pet

If the family has Fifi (cat) and Pickle (dog), each gets its own portrait when its card surfaces. Stat rings adapt: dogs have Walk / Food / Water; cats have Hunger / Litter / Water. The category is "routine"; the surface adapts.

Setup (parent)

Same as elsewhere: "Add a pet" → name + species + which kids look after them. Behind the cards, this drives the same `house_details.pets[]` data we already have. The schedule is just a daily card; complex day-of-week schedules become a small "Cadence" toggle: Daily / Every other / Weekends only. Three options collapse the current 7-day matrix.

A ring. A nudge. Four device chips.

The ring is the rules

Screen time is the most rules-laden surface in the current app — device × day × hours × chore-gate × age. Here it's one ring and four chips. The ring shows total minutes earned today. The chips show how much is left in each device bucket. That's the rules.

The ring fills as cards complete

Each chore card swiped done adds minutes to the ring (rate configurable per family — 15 min / chore by default). The kid sees the ring fill in real time as they complete cards. The bridge from "did chores" → "earned time" is felt as a continuous animation, not announced as a rule.

VR is a chip with a day-of-week label

The VR chip is greyed out and reads "Sat" — meaning weekends only. On Saturday and Sunday it lights up with minutes available. No nested settings; the chip's state IS the rule. Similar pattern for any restricted device (school nights = console locked, etc).

Tap a chip = launch the device

Tap the Console chip → iOS Screen Time unlocks the games on the kid's iPad / paired Apple TV. The minutes count down in the chip live. When 0:00, the chip greys out and a card slides into the main stack: "Time's up. Earn 30 min more by finishing 2 cards."

Chore-gate, made gentle

The current "must complete chores before screen time" toggle becomes a card that reads "Three more cards before the ring unlocks." The kid sees the locked ring greyed; chores fill it in. No dialog says "you can't" — the ring just shows you can't, which is a much softer no.

Settings (parent)

One screen, four toggles per kid:

  • Earn rate (default 15m / chore — can dial up or down).
  • Daily cap (default 2h weekday, 3h weekend).
  • VR cadence (None / Weekends / Daily).
  • Chore-gate on or off (defaults: on).

The whole family on one calm screen.

One deck per family member

The parent's home screen is a small rack of decks. One row per kid, plus the parent's own row at the bottom if they participate. Each row shows: the kid's "spine" (a coloured card-edge), name, today's ratio, and a single thin progress bar. The whole family in 5 seconds.

Approvals as a single black strip

The pending approvals queue is one black strip at the top with a red badge count and a one-line summary of what's waiting. Tap → slide-up sheet of approval cards (same card primitive: each pending approval is a card, swipe right = approve, swipe left = needs redo).

No tabs

The whole parent app is the rack. Tap a deck → see that kid's cards. Tap "The Haridys" header → family settings. Long-press a deck → quick action: issue a one-off chore for that kid. Five gestures cover everything that's currently spread across Command Center / Approvals / Contracts / Shop / Settings.

Trust over surveillance

The parent's deck view is intentionally shallow. They see ratio + bar. They don't see exactly which chore each kid skipped without drilling. The design assumption is that the kid is doing fine unless a deck is conspicuously low. Healthy defaults; healthy surface.

Multi-parent

Both parents see the same rack. Approvals are first-come — once one parent approves a card, it disappears from the other's strip. No "who claimed this" question; the approval just lands. Soft co-parenting baked in.

Settings, hidden but findable

Tap the family name at the very top → a small settings sheet: invite codes, screen-time defaults, contracts, billing. Nothing in the main interface advertises itself.

What's still on the table

  • Rewards / shop → a separate "spend" gesture: pull down on the home stack and the shop card slides in as a peek. Items rendered as small minimal cards with point cost. Buying = tap, with confirmation.
  • Pitched contracts → the kid composes a card. "Title. Description. Asking 50 stars." The card lands in the parent's approvals strip. Same primitive, sender different.
  • Skills profile → six small numbers in a settings sheet on the kid's profile. The Card concept consciously de-emphasizes gamification — skills are present for the kids who want them, invisible to those who don't.
  • Streaks / badges → mostly cut. The Card concept's calm aesthetic is at odds with badge-collecting. A single line of "12 day streak" appears at the top of the stack on day milestones (7, 30, 90), then disappears.
