Synthesis · the recommended direction

Concept 04

The Living Garden.

Your feedback was: Garden wins for soul, but the Card stack at the bottom is what actually drives a kid through the day. The pet idea works, but real-world pets and "garden creatures" need to stay distinct. Screen time can't be all-or-nothing — different families play differently, and "finish all chores before any play" is unrealistic on a Tuesday afternoon. And the whole thing should feel alive — wind, fluttering, a cat that wanders, fireflies at dusk.

Concept 04 takes the Garden's identity and folds in the things you asked for. Below: nine screens, a navigation map, a setup wizard, and a proposed play-time system that respects how families actually live.

Steve Jobs's principle wasn't minimalism — it was synthesis. The iPhone was three things in one thing. The Living Garden is the calm of the Garden, the focus of the Card, and a play-time system that doesn't pretend kids only play in the evening.

The Jobs Edit

First pass of the Living Garden was Jobs-shallow — I picked aesthetics and called it design. A real Jobs review of this concept would be sharper. Below: the cuts a Jobs-led product would force, the framing that should drive the marketing, the single demo moment, the silence at the end. Then the rest of the page (mockups, screens) — already shipped, but to be re-cut against this doctrine.

What this product is

OMyDays is not a chore tracker. It is the first family ritual app — a small living world your kids tend daily, alongside breakfast, dinner, and bedtime. The chore is the watering. The watering is the ritual.

"Chore" never appears in kid-facing UI. Not on a card. Not on a button. Not in a notification. Garden, flower, water, seed, ripe — that is the vocabulary, end to end. Marketing copy: "Plant a garden with your family." Not "the smartest chore app for families."

What gets cut after a real Jobs review

The current Living Garden draft (the rest of this page) still carries holdover habits from the gamified app. A real edit pass cuts deeper:

What's still in the draftWhy it goes
The skills profile (Discipline / Reliability / Initiative / etc) The garden IS the skill profile. Tree size, foliage density, wildlife population. Six bars are off-metaphor.
Reward chips on cards ("+1 🌱") The flower opening is the reward. Don't double-bill it. Seed count ticks up silently in the top corner.
Five zone chips at the bottom Three at most: 🏡 Home · 🐾 Animals · ⛲ Fountain. Wildlife glade is reachable from inside the garden by tapping a critter. Profile doesn't exist as a destination.
The 6-screen setup wizard Two screens. "Family name and your name." "Add a kid." Done. Everything else has a defensible default; defaults ship, options come later.
The 7-row Codex Two rows: Family + Account. Everything else lives where it lives. Pet rotation is set inside Animal Corner. Play caps are set inside the Fountain. Settings only exist when you're already in the place.
The "Strict mode" toggle Gone. Pick one model — earn rate — and ship it. Families that hate it can complain on day 3. We then have feedback worth listening to. Don't cover both bets in v1.
Per-kid play overrides v1 is family-wide. v2 if anyone asks.
Day-of-week pet schedules Daily, rotated among the kids the parent ticked. One toggle. The "litter every other day" family customizes via a single "Cadence" preset, not a 7-day grid.
The word "chore" anywhere kids see Use "today's flowers" or "what's blooming." Cards are typed by colour and emoji, not by category labels.
Bottom-edge swipe-up hint copy The kid will discover swipe in the first 5 seconds. Trust them. Hint text is for tutorials Jobs would refuse to ship.

The first-frame moment

A new kid signs in. Black screen. A single dot of light at centre. A note swells. The dot sprouts into a sapling. Sky paints itself in. Sound design fades in — wind, distant birdsong. "Yusuf — this is your garden." Tap to continue. The cat walks in from the side, sits, watches. "Fifi lives here too." Tap. Three flower buds appear around the tree. "These are today." Tap. A card slides up from the bottom: brush teeth. "Water this one to see what happens." The kid does. First-run is over.

Total: 35 seconds. No form. No tab. No options. No "Welcome to OMyDays!" onboarding card. The first thing the kid feels is being addressed by name in their own garden, with a creature already living there, awaiting them.

The demo moment

If a journalist asks for the one thing that defines the product, this is what gets recorded:

  1. The kid swipes the card up.
  2. Three water droplets shoot from the swipe and arc upward to the corresponding flower.
  3. The flower opens (0.4s spring) with a soft ting, a medium haptic, and a ripple ring that lingers for 5 seconds.
  4. The card stack underneath compacts; the next card slides up.
  5. Top corner: seeds count, in a slow odometer roll, ticks up by 1.

That sequence is the entire product. 600 milliseconds. Every other surface in the app is context for that gesture. The 50 hours of polish budget go here first.

The silence at the end of the day

Last card watered. Card stack empty. Wind sweeps through the garden. Two birds settle in the tree. The tree visibly grows by 1.5% (perceptible over a week, not a day). Sky tilts toward dusk. The app stays silent. No "well done!" toast. No "what's next?" prompt. No notification asking for an app review. The screen just sits there, alive, until the kid puts the phone down.

That silence is the most expensive thing in the design. Every product manager will want to fill it with a celebration screen, a leaderboard update, a streak meter pulse. Jobs's job, in the room, is to defend the silence.

What this means for the parent app

Most days a parent should not open the app. Approvals come as iOS push notifications styled like text messages — a single tap from the lock screen approves; a long-press shows the kid's note ("Took her round the block, she met a labrador"). The Family Park view exists for the rare moments parents want the overview — not as the home screen.

The Codex is also intentionally undersold. A parent should configure once, ever — at setup. After that, the app should be self-running. If a parent finds themselves opening the Codex twice in a month, we have a defaults problem, not a UI problem.

What this means for the build plan below

The 4-phase plan at the bottom of this page still stands, but each phase is smaller after the cuts:

  • Phase 1 ships without skills, without zone-chip nav, without per-card reward chips.
  • Phase 2 ships one play model, family-wide. The strict mode toggle is dropped.
  • Phase 3 ships a 2-row Codex, not a 7-row one. Settings move into the places they describe.
  • Phase 4 — the aliveness — gets more budget, because it's no longer competing with skill bars and reward chips for engineering time. The animations are the product.

Net build estimate: ~3.5 weeks instead of 5, because half of what I drew was scope I'd argue out of the room.

The garden is alive

Five things on this screen are moving subtly: the tree sways in the breeze, a butterfly drifts toward a flower, the cat (Fifi) wanders across the grass, a small bird flits in from the left, the grass ripples. Nothing is loud. Nothing demands attention. The screen is alive even when the kid isn't doing anything. That's the soul piece you asked for.

The card pulls you forward

The bottom of the screen always shows ONE card — today's next chore. Swipe it up to water → particles fall onto the corresponding flower in the garden, the flower opens into bloom, a soft chime + haptic fires, and the next card slides up from below. Swiping is watering. The two metaphors fuse.

The garden tells the day's story

Today's flowers are sized big and bright. Yesterday's flowers are smaller, off to the sides — they're permanent, they're growing the garden's history. Watered flowers have a subtle ripple animation (you can see it on the toothbrush flower above). Unwatered flowers are dimmer, awaiting their card.

Long-press the card → see the spread

Hold the card for 0.4s and the stack opens into a list of all today's cards. Tap any to bring it to the front out of order. That's the only "list view" in the entire app. (Mockup is the next screen below.)

Time of day shifts the scene

The background gradient changes through the day: dawn at 6am, day from 9am, dusk from 5pm, night from 9pm. Visual difference is subtle (a 10% color shift) — but kids feel the difference between "morning chores" and "evening routine" without being told.

The list view that hides itself

By default, the kid sees one card. When they want to peek ahead or skip around, they long-press and the stack expands into the spread above. Color-coded by category (yellow = habit, green = routine, purple = pitched). Tap any row → that card jumps to the front.

Real pets live in the Animal Corner

Tap the 🐾 zone icon → Fifi's meadow. She wanders. She speaks (a small bubble shows her current need). The three care rows below are her actual chores from the data layer — feeding, litter, water — but they appear here as her requests, with sibling history shown ("Done by Sara at 7:42 yesterday").

This is where the duty lives

Real-world animal duties happen here and only here. They also surface as cards in the home screen's stack — those two things are the same data, two views. When you complete a duty here, the card on the home screen disappears too.

Multi-pet meadow

If the family has multiple pets, the meadow is wider and they each occupy a corner. Wandering animations make it feel like a small farm. Tapping a pet brings them centre.

The shop is the glade

The "shop" isn't a separate tab. It's a place — the wildlife glade. You walk into it. You see the animals you've already brought home wandering. You see the ones you could buy. Buying one is adopting — they show up in your home garden the next time you go back, mingling with the tree, alongside Fifi (who is unmistakably special because she eats and meows).

Visual differentiation in the home garden

Garden critters get a faint pastel glow (you can see hedgehogs in the home screen sometimes). Real pets like Fifi do not — they look photographic by comparison. A 6-year-old will pick it up in a day.

The shop also sells flowers and trees

Beyond animals, the glade also sells flower types (unlocking new flower-emoji palettes for chore types) and tree skins (cherry blossom, maple, pine). Cosmetics that make the garden personal without changing the data layer.

Cash rewards from contracts

Real money rewards (the £10 cash for "wash the car") stay outside the seeds economy entirely. Cash shows up in a small purse in the topbar when relevant — tap to see balance. Seeds are the in-app currency; cash is the out-of-app reward. Two distinct things.

The proposal: earn rate, no hard gate

The current model is binary: chores done = play unlocked, otherwise locked. That's too crude — your example of "evening teeth-brushing blocks afternoon play" is exactly right.

The new model has two dials:

DialWhat it doesFamily default
Earn rate Each completed chore adds N minutes to the pond 15 min / chore
Daily cap Max minutes the pond can hold today 2h weekday, 3h weekend

So at 4pm with 6 chores done, Yusuf has 90 minutes of pond time even though evening chores haven't happened yet. Evening chores top him up later. Future chores never block current play. If a kid does no chores all day, the pond's empty — that's the only gate.

Optional strict mode

For families that want the harder rule (we've met them), there's a "must finish morning chain before screen time" toggle in setup. Defaults to off. Most families will find the earn-rate model sufficient because the natural friction is real: empty pond = no play.

Rooms are family-customised

What "play" means varies wildly. Some families have Console + PC + VR. Some have outdoor games and a TV. Some play together, some don't. Setup asks each family which rooms exist for them — and the fountain shows only those.

Setup wizard for play (next screen)

The setup flow asks four questions and configures the rooms, rates, caps, and weekend rules from there. Six taps, no matrix. See the next mockup.

Special: VR portal

VR is treated as more precious than other devices. Default rule: weekends only, after-morning-chores. The portal in the fountain's quartet shows a glow when available, locked text when not. Many families will find this default appropriate — those who don't, toggle it off in setup.

Special: family time room

If the parent ticks "Telly" or "Family games" in setup, that room is marked "free, with family" — meaning it doesn't draw from the pond. The kid can sit on the sofa with mum or dad without the chore-gate kicking in. This handles your "play together" case explicitly.

Edge cases worth noting
  • If pond is empty, rooms show "Earn 15m by watering 1 flower". One nudge sentence, no lecture.
  • School days vs weekend caps differ — set in setup once.
  • Sick day mode: parent can grant 1h "free pond" for one day from the parent codex.
  • Outdoor play: a special room "Outside" that the kid can tap as "I'm going outside" — earns extra seeds, is parent-trust based, no enforcement. Optional.

The full setup, one question per screen

  1. Family name + a parent's first name. One field.
  2. Add the kids. Tap + for each kid, name + age. (5s per kid.)
  3. How does your family play? ← shown above. Multi-select. Determines the fountain's rooms.
  4. Earn rate. Slider: "Each chore = how many minutes of play?" Default 15. Range 5–30.
  5. Daily caps. Two sliders: weekday max / weekend max.
  6. Pets. Add Fifi & co. Same as the current flow but visual.

That's the whole setup. Six screens. Zero matrix UIs. Zero day-of-week grids in the middle of onboarding.

The "Strict mode" toggle is buried

The "must complete morning chores before any screen time" rule lives in the Codex (parent settings) under "Advanced rules". By default it's off. Families who want it find it; families who don't never see it.

Per-kid customisation comes later

The wizard sets family-wide defaults so onboarding stays fast. After setup, parents can fine-tune per-kid (different cap for the 6-year-old vs the 12-year-old) from the Codex. Most won't bother. Defaults take you 90% of the way.

Onboarding for the kid

Separate, lighter flow when the kid logs in for the first time: "Pick your tree" (3 styles), "Pick your seed colour" (3 hues), "Meet Fifi" (animation). 30 seconds. They land in their living garden.

Everything else, in one place

Seven rows. The Codex contains every advanced or rare-use setting — none of which a parent should have to find more than once a month. Family members. Play defaults. Pets. Schedules. Contracts. Advanced rules. Billing.

"Advanced rules" is where strictness lives

This is where a parent enables strict mode (chores must be done before play), age gates (different caps per kid), sick days, future inter-family leaderboard opt-in, etc. Buried by design — defaults cover 80% of families.

Naming choices

"Codex" not "Settings". "Play time defaults" not "Screen time rules". "Family members" not "User management". Every word is chosen to feel like a parent's tool, not an administrator's console. Same data underneath; different language above.

Navigation: how zones connect
