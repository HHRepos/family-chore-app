Concept 01

The Garden.

Your child does not "do chores". Your child grows a garden. Each chore waters a flower. Each week of consistency thickens the tree's branches. Each pet is a real animal that lives in their plot. Each hour of rest is earned and visualized as the still water of a pond they sit beside. After a month, your child has a landscape they built. After a year, they have a forest.

The fundamental Steve Jobs move isn't minimalism. It's changing the metaphor. The original Mac wasn't a faster terminal — it was a desk. OMyDays isn't a better chore tracker — it's a garden.

Below: four screens covering the full app surface. Daily chores (home), pet management (animal corner), gaming/screen-time/VR rules (rest by the fountain), and the parent's family-wide view (the family park).

The whole app, on one screen.

What's on screen, what isn't

The screen contains: a tree, three flowers, your name, the day count, a seed counter (the only number), and a single suggested action card. That's it. No tabs, no leaderboard, no XP bar, no streak meter, no badges grid.

The tree IS the streak. Its size and density grow visibly as the days accumulate. After 30 days the kid sees a different tree than after 7. After 90, it's lush and unrecognizable.

Interaction

Tap a flower → water particles fall → the flower opens into a brighter bloom → a soft chime + gentle haptic. The flower then stays in the garden permanently. Yesterday's flowers are still there, slightly smaller; today's are vivid; tomorrow's are buds waiting.

Edge cases
  • Missed day = a single brown leaf on the tree. Not a streak reset, just a memory. Recoverable next day.
  • More than 5 chores on a busy day = flowers cluster. The kid scrolls a tiny amount; no list view ever appears.
  • Empty / first-day state = a single seed in soil. "Tap to plant your first flower." Onboarding becomes a moment, not a flow.
What we cut
  • Tabs (now 0)
  • Points / XP / level / level-name (now: seeds + day count)
  • Streak meter, weekly badges, monthly badges (now: the tree itself)
  • Leaderboard (moved into family park, parent-only)

Pets are characters, not config rows.

The pet is alive

Pets aren't checkbox configurations. Fifi is a real character on a real meadow. When she needs care, she shows up and tells you. A speech bubble. A nervous tail flick. Sometimes she sits patiently because someone else already fed her — and the row shows who and when, so siblings see each other's care.

Why this matters

A 6-year-old will care for a creature that "feels like" a creature. They will not care for a row labeled "Feed cat (Fifi)" with a rotation_children: [...] schedule underneath. The data layer doesn't change — the metaphor does.

Rotation, made human

The "rotation" we currently configure with checkboxes becomes ambient: the row says "Today's your turn" or "Sara fed her at 7:42". Parent doesn't set up a rotation in a config screen — they just tap which kids "look after" Fifi in setup, the app rotates automatically.

Multiple pets

Each pet gets its own meadow. Cat in tall grass. Dog by the gate. Bird in the tree. The home screen's tree shrinks slightly when you enter the corner so you see it's all the same garden.

Setup (parent)

"Add a pet" → name + species + which kids look after them. That's all the config a parent ever has to do. Days-of-the-week schedule from the current app collapses into "every day, takes turns". For specialty cases (litter on alternate days), parent toggles a "less often" option per task — three preset cadences, no day grid.

Screen time as a place you visit, not a number you spend.

Screen time isn't a budget. It's a place.

The current app has parental rules with day-of-week, hours-per-day, device-type configuration. Functional, but soulless. Here, screen time becomes "rest by the fountain" — a place you go after a day's work in the garden.

The pond is the point

The pond visibly fills as the kid completes flowers. Each completed chore raises the water level. If the pond is empty, there is nothing to spend. If the pond is full, the kid sees lit-up still water and feels they earned it. The bridge from "did chores" to "earned screen time" stops being a parental rule and becomes a felt, visible truth.

VR as a special pavilion

VR isn't just "a more expensive tablet". It's a separate room — the starlight pavilion — visible only on weekends or after a streak. Parents set the rules once: "VR weekends only, max 1h/day, only after morning chores." The kid sees the pavilion locked or unlocked, no nagging.

Per-device buckets

Four cards — TV, Console, Tablet, VR. Each shows time remaining. Tap to "enter" — actually launches the app's screen-time gate. Below: the pond's water level falls in real time as they play (since the iOS Screen Time API can hand us minutes).

Rules the parent sets, in plain language
  • "Each chore = 15 min of pond time" (the rate, configurable per kid)
  • "VR on weekends only"
  • "No screens before chores done" (chore-gate, already in the backend)
  • "Max 2h on weekdays" (cap)

Four toggles. No day grid. No matrix UI. The complexity of the current rules screen collapses because most families want the same defaults.

Approval as harvest, not as bureaucracy.

Parents are park keepers, not auditors

The current parent flow is a list of "approve / reject" buttons. It feels like reviewing expense reports. Here, every kid's plot lives next to the others. A completed-but-unapproved chore appears as a ripe fruit — visually charming, gently insistent, but not nagging.

The "ripe" gesture

Tap a plot → see today's flowers. Approve = pick the fruit (a small plucking gesture, satisfying haptic). Reject = let it fall back to a bud (rare, used only when something genuinely wasn't done). The verb "reject" disappears entirely.

Multi-kid at-a-glance

Each kid's tree size, today's progress bar, today's number of ripe fruits. Five seconds and you have the whole family's morning. No drilling into separate tabs.

Parent participating in chores

If the parent has opted into rotation (current toggle), their own plot appears at the bottom — smaller, subtitled "Optional rotation". Their progress is for themselves; their flowers don't need approval. The ambient social pressure of the kids' plots beside theirs is enough.

Settings, hidden where they should be

The current Settings tab disappears entirely. Tap a kid's plot and hold → the management view: invite codes, role changes, pet assignments. Tap the family name at top → family-wide settings: bin schedule, screen-time defaults, contracts. Three taps deep instead of front-and-center.

What's still on the table

The Garden handles the four core surfaces above. It also subsumes:

  • Rewards / shop → "the nursery". Spend seeds on new flower types to plant. Each new flower category is a permanent garden upgrade. (Cash rewards from contracts stay as a sibling feature, just visually styled as "found a coin in the garden".)
  • Contracts / pitched jobs → "barter at the garden gate". Older kid drags a special flower bud to the gate to offer it; the parent waters or declines. Naming changes; flow stays.
  • Skills profile → the tree's foliage shows skill density. Discipline = trunk thickness. Responsibility = root system (visible in a soil cross-section). Initiative = wildflowers around the tree. Visual; not a tab.
  • Bin collection rotation → a wheelbarrow that moves between plots once a week. Whoever's plot it sits on does the bins. Visual rotation, no table.
