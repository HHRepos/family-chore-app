A design proposal

OMyDays. Stripped to the bone.

The current app has 5 tabs, 6 sub-skills, 8 stat tiles, 3 reward systems, a weekly badge ladder, a streak meter, an XP bar, a leaderboard, a contracts board, a rules screen, a settings tree, an avatar customizer and a points pop animation. It works. It's not memorable. A 7-year-old won't tell their friends about it.

Three concepts below. Each picks a single metaphor and ruthlessly removes everything that doesn't serve it. They are not three variations — they are three different bets on what this product is.

"The hardest part of design is not the part you put in. It's the part you leave out." Each of these leaves out 70% of what's currently on screen.

Concept 1 — child's home screen

01

The Garden

You don't do chores. You grow yourself. Every chore you complete waters a flower. Every week you stay consistent, the tree grows. After a month you have a whole landscape that's yours, that you built.

Metaphor

A living garden you are responsible for. Tending the garden is the chore; the garden grows because of you.

What disappears
  • Tabs. There is one screen. Everything you need is on it.
  • Points, XP, levels — replaced by a single number: how many days you've watered. The tree is the score.
  • Badge grids, streak meters, weekly ladders — the tree's size IS the badge.
  • The shop, for now. Rewards re-enter as seeds for new plants you can grow.
The moment of joy

Tap a flower → water particles fall → the flower opens into bloom → a soft chime, gentle haptic. The flower stays in your garden forever. After 30 days you walk in to a riot of color you made.

Family layer

The "parent" view is the family park: your kids' gardens side by side. Approval = mowing the path between them so the family can walk through each other's gardens. Co-watering for shared chores (bins, pets).

CalmPatientBeautifulLong-arc
Inspiration & references
  • Animal Crossing — accumulation as story
  • Headspace — color-as-mood, breathing animations
  • Forest (the focus app) — single growing thing as metric

Tone: hand-drawn watercolor. Heavily illustrated. No neon.
Best for: families that already feel a little frantic. Reduces anxiety.

Concept 2 — child's home screen

02

The Quest

Every day is an adventure. Your child has a hero, a class, a level, and a map. Today's chores are quest pins on the map. Boss fights are challenge tasks the parent posts. Co-op is when siblings team up on a task.

Metaphor

Pokémon-style overworld. Your character. Your inventory. Your story arc that gets bigger every day.

What disappears
  • The word "chore" (it's a quest now).
  • The five tabs (replaced by zones on a single map: Home, Pets, Kitchen, Outside).
  • Points become coins, XP becomes XP — kids already speak this language fluently.
  • The leaderboard becomes the family party — you can see your siblings' avatars on the map.
The moment of joy

Quest complete → "+8 ★" floats up → chime → your character LEVELS UP (visible animation, fanfare). A new piece of armor unlocks. You walk to your bed feeling 6 inches taller.

Family layer

Parent's view is "Quest Master Mode" — they're the dungeon master. They can post side quests (the contracts board), bless boss fights, award rare loot. The family forms a "party" — parent helps a child complete a tough quest = co-op XP for both.

AdventurousStory-drivenStickyIdentity
Inspiration & references
  • Pokémon Scarlet/Violet — overworld with markers
  • Habitica — chore-as-RPG, but it never crossed the bridge to fun
  • Stardew Valley — living world that progresses with you

Tone: pixel-art rendered crisp on retina. CRT-modern.
Best for: families with multiple kids. Sibling rivalry becomes sibling co-op.

Concept 3 — child's home screen

03

The Card

Your day is a stack of cards. The top card is the only thing that matters. Do it. Swipe up. The next card slides into place. The whole app is one screen, one card, one tap.

Metaphor

The Things 3 / iA Writer school of design. Premium minimal. The product is a beautiful, calm tool — not a game, not a garden. A tool that respects your time and your attention.

What disappears
  • Everything except today's stack of cards.
  • Numbers. The only number on screen is "3 / 5" — how many cards left.
  • Color, mostly. Cream paper, black ink, one accent color per card category.
  • The tab bar. Pull down for shop, side-swipe for family — but only when you actively need them.
The moment of joy

Big satisfying swipe + heavy haptic + a single warm "ting". The stack of remaining cards visibly shrinks. When you finish the last card, the screen goes blank with one line: "That's all today. Well done." And then, intentionally, nothing.

Family layer

Each family member is a deck. The parent's home screen is a small rack of deck-spines along the side. Tap a child's deck to peek at what they're doing. Nothing more — the parent is invited to trust, not surveil.

FrictionlessPremiumCalmAdult-feeling
Inspiration & references
  • Things 3 — the gold standard of considered iOS design
  • iA Writer — typography as the entire experience
  • Tinder — swipe physics done with respect, not gimmick
  • Apple Reminders + Streaks — minimal but tactile

Tone: warm cream paper, sharp black, tactile shadows.
Best for: older kids (10+) and parents who want the app to get out of the way.

If you put me in a room and made me pick

The Garden, then layer in the Quest as a "mode".

The Garden has a soul. It changes how a kid thinks about the app — from "I have to do my chores" to "I have to look after my garden". That's the Jobs move: it's not a chore app, it's a thing you grow.

The Quest is the right add-on for older kids who outgrow the calm — offer it as a switchable theme later (an iOS Settings toggle, like switching wallpapers). Same data underneath, different skin.

The Card concept is genuinely the most "Steve" — but it's adult-feeling. Ship it later as a Parent Mode for adults running their own self-care. Kids won't fall in love with it.

Phase 1 (4–6 weeks) Re-skin the existing app to The Garden. Same data layer, same backend, new visual language. Single screen. Drop 4 of 5 tabs. Move Skills/Shop/Schedule into a pull-up tray.
Phase 2 (2 weeks) Add the Quest theme switcher. Kids 8+ unlock it as a "level 5 reward". Adds the map, the pixel character, the loot animation — nothing else.
Phase 3 (later) Card mode for parents. Pull-down gesture from anywhere lights up a clean card stack of parent-side tasks (approve, restock rewards, plan next week). It is for the adults.

Three things I'd insist on regardless of which we pick

  • Sound design. Every chore completion has a sound. Different per category. They will become the audio fingerprint of your kid's day.
  • Haptics. The "thunk" when a chore lands is felt, not heard. Borrow Apple's Haptic Touch playbook — distinct patterns for different events.
  • One typeface, two weights, one accent colour. The current design uses 6 neon colours, 4 fonts, and 8 button styles. That isn't kid-friendly — it's noisy. A child reads emotion from clarity, not from candy.

Mockups are CSS-rendered, not implemented. No JS, no images. Browser-inspect to see how every pixel is composed. Tell me which one resonates and I'll spec the build.
